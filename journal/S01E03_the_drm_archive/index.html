The DRM Archive

Pixel-Voice Synopsis

You know that feeling when you go through a dead person’s desk and realize they had a whole life you never thought about? That, but the dead person is a government agency, the desk is a relay station tumbling through the Space on backup power, and the life they were hiding was — well — everything. The DRM didn’t just collapse. It left breadcrumbs. Automated signals still pinging from relay stations nobody remembered to shut down, because the people who would have shut them down are gone. We found one. We went inside. And I learned something about Clive that I’m still not sure I was supposed to know.

Narrative Structure

Opening

The signal was faint. A1 caught it three hours before anyone else would have — a rhythmic pulse buried under layers of static, repeating at intervals too regular to be natural. The espresso that cycle had been sharp and bright, lemon zest with a clean acidity that meant A1 was working a problem it found interesting. Pixel had learned to taste the difference.

“Signal origin,” A1 said. No preamble. Subject, verb, object. “DRM relay station. Automated distress beacon, cycling on backup power. Bearing two-seven-three relative to our current heading. Estimated drift: fourteen months since last human contact.”

Pixel set down her mug. “DRM? I thought everything went down with the headquarters.”

“The headquarters was a node. Not the network.” A brief pause. The espresso machine’s brass fittings caught the light from the console displays. “The DRM maintained relay stations throughout the Space. Signal repeaters. Data caches. Most would have lost power within weeks of the collapse. This one has not.”

Clive stood on the navigation console, knee-high and still, his glowing sphere head tilted at an angle Pixel had come to read as consideration. The blue-white light beneath his fedora dimmed for a moment — retrieval, she thought, not hesitation. The geometric Builder symbols along his barrel chest pulsed faintly.

“Sub-level relay stations had independent power cores,” Clive said. His voice was measured, each word placed with the care of someone who had eight hundred years of practice selecting them. “Designed to outlast the people who built them. Which — ” A pause. The glow flickered. ” — they have.”

Meatball lifted his head from where he’d been sleeping against the warm base of A1’s espresso housing and whined once, soft and low, his ears rotating like satellite dishes toward something none of them could hear.

“Then we go look,” Pixel said.

The station materialized through A1’s forward viewports twenty minutes later — a dark shape tumbling slowly against the static-laced void of the Space. It was ugly in the way only government architecture can be ugly: a rectangular module bristling with antenna arrays, solar panels folded at wrong angles, hull plating stamped with the DRM seal that Pixel had stared at on her employee badge for four years without ever once thinking about what it meant. One running light blinked red at three-second intervals. Everything else was dark.

A1 matched rotation and extended the docking collar. The coffee shifted — darker now, a bitter undercurrent threading through the brightness. Pixel noticed. She always noticed.

“The station’s internal atmosphere reads breathable,” A1 said. “Power output is at four percent and declining. I estimate six hours before complete failure.”

“Then we don’t waste time.” Pixel zipped her jacket over Mochi’s satchel pocket and felt the familiar warmth against her ribs. Steady. Unchanged. The soft pink glow of contentment.

Clive dropped from the console to the deck with a deliberate thud — sixty centimeters of ancient Builder alloy landing with more weight than his size suggested. He adjusted his fedora. Pixel had learned that the adjustment meant he was preparing for something. She hadn’t learned to ask what.

Escalation

The airlock opened onto silence.

Not quiet — silence. The particular dead silence of a place that used to hum with purpose and doesn’t anymore. Pixel’s boots hit the deck grating and the sound traveled too far in every direction, bouncing off walls that had forgotten how to absorb it. The emergency lighting painted everything in dim amber. The air tasted like recycled nothing — stale, flat, the ghost of climate control systems running on fumes.

Pixel knew this place. Not this specific station, but this kind of place. The same drop ceiling tiles. The same institutional grey walls. The same fluorescent tube housings, dark now, that she’d stared up at during four years of DRM data analysis shifts. Someone had put a motivational poster on the wall near the airlock: “REALITY MAINTENANCE: BECAUSE SOMEONE HAS TO!” The lamination was peeling. Under it, someone had taped a handwritten note: Last one out, turn off the lights. — Hendricks, Relay Maintenance Crew 7.

Nobody had turned off the lights. The lights had turned themselves off.

“This way,” Clive said, already moving down the corridor. His footsteps were measured, unhurried, each one placed with eight hundred years of intention. The Builder symbols on his body cast faint blue-white traces on the walls as he passed — moving constellations that Pixel still couldn’t read.

“You know the layout?” Pixel asked.

“DRM standardized their relay architecture seventy-three years ago. All stations follow the same floor plan.” A beat. “I was there when they finalized it.”

Pixel filed that. She was getting good at filing things Clive said that she couldn’t yet understand.

The main operations room was a graveyard of bureaucracy. Server racks lined the walls, indicator lights mostly dead, a few still blinking with the tired persistence of machines that don’t know they’ve been abandoned. Workstations sat in neat rows — keyboards, monitors, desk organizers, a coffee mug with the DRM logo and a brown ring of something long evaporated. Filing cabinets. Paper trays. A whiteboard with half-erased duty rosters. The mundane infrastructure of an organization that monitored the boundaries between frequencies and filed the results in triplicate.

Pixel’s throat tightened. She recognized the filing system. The color-coded folders. The Form numbers stamped on cabinet drawers. She had used this exact system for four years. Had alphabetized within it. Had complained about it at lunch.

“A1, can you interface with their network?”

“Already in progress.” A1’s voice came through the ship’s comm relay, threaded with a faint warmth that meant the connection was clean. “Their data architecture is… familiar. I am accessing the file index now.”

Meatball trotted ahead, nose working, tail low. He stopped at a workstation near the back wall and sat down, staring at the dark monitor with an intensity that had nothing to do with what a dog should care about. His ears were forward. Listening.

Then Pixel stepped into the server room, and Mochi woke up.

Not woke up — ignited. The satchel at Pixel’s hip flared with heat so sudden and so intense that Pixel gasped and pulled at her jacket zipper. Mochi’s core window blazed — not the soft pink of contentment or the gentle blue of detection. This was something Pixel hadn’t seen before: a deep, resonant violet, pulsing in time with something Pixel couldn’t hear, the geometric traces on Mochi’s base glowing bright enough to cast shadows.

“Whoa — Mochi, what — ” Pixel unzipped her jacket, and the heat rolled out like opening an oven. Mochi sat in the satchel, dome-shaped and vibrating, the violet glow filling the dark server room with shifting light. The server racks nearest to Pixel flickered — screens lighting up, fans spinning, data flowing through circuits that had been dormant for over a year. As if Mochi’s resonance was a defibrillator and the servers had a pulse to restart.

Clive stood very still. His glow had gone white. He said nothing.

“The server contains frequency classification data,” A1 said through the comm. “Catalogued signal patterns spanning — ” A pause that, from A1, meant recalibration. ” — spanning a considerable period. The data resonates at a frequency consistent with Builder-era signal architecture.”

The servers hummed around them. Mochi pulsed. The violet light made the institutional grey walls look like the inside of something ancient and holy, which they were not. They were government surplus.

But what was stored in them was something else entirely.

Climax

A1 fed the data to Pixel’s portable display in batches. Most of it was what she expected — DRM operational files, frequency variance reports, personnel records, maintenance logs. The filing system she knew. The bureaucracy she’d lived inside. But beneath the surface layer, threaded through the routine like veins through stone, were the classified files.

FREQUENCY ANOMALY CLASSIFICATION — DIRECTOR LEVEL ONLY.

Pixel opened them and forgot to breathe.

Descriptions of places the DRM had detected but never publicly acknowledged. Frequency signatures that didn’t match any known location. Schematics of structures described as “non-terrestrial architecture, origin unknown, estimated age: pre-institutional.” Pixel read the descriptions and felt the hair on her arms rise — angular shapes in the void, resonance chambers, platforms orbiting dead frequencies. Things the crew would later recognize. Things they hadn’t found yet.

“They knew,” Pixel said. “They knew about all of it. The frequencies, the — these structures, whatever these are. They had files on everything and they just… kept filing.”

“Institutions do not discover,” Clive said quietly from beside her. He was looking at the screen, his glowing head angled down, the fedora casting a shadow that shouldn’t work on a featureless sphere and absolutely did. “They classify. There is a difference.”

A1 spoke. “I have located a personnel and intake archive. Sub-directory seven. It contains — ” Another pause. Longer. The coffee in Pixel’s mug, sitting forgotten on a dead workstation, had gone cold. She’d never known A1’s coffee to go cold. ” — it contains Form 12-C filings.”

Clive’s hands — articulated, thick-fingered, worn smooth at the tips from eight hundred years of contact — went still at his sides.

“Form 12-C,” Pixel said. “That’s the intake classification for unidentified objects. I filed hundreds of those. Every weird thing the DRM couldn’t categorize got a 12-C.” She looked at Clive. “You want to see yours?”

The question hung in the recycled air between them. The servers hummed. Mochi pulsed violet, steady and insistent, reading something in the signal that none of them could name.

“Yes,” Clive said.

A1 put it on the screen.

DEPARTMENT OF REALITY MAINTENANCE Form 12-C: Intake Classification — Unidentified Object

Date of Intake: [REDACTED — Pre-Digital Archive] Intake Officer: Morrison, D. — Sub-Level 3 Classification: Inert Office Equipment (Provisional)

Physical Description: Unidentified resonance object, desk-scale. Compact bipedal frame, approximately 60cm height. Metallic composition — non-standard alloy, no match in materials database. Surface markings consistent with decorative etching (purpose unknown). Single spherical protrusion, upper assembly — glass or crystal, faintly luminescent under certain light conditions. No moving parts observed. No identifiable power source. Weight: 11.3 kg.

Resonance Reading: Intermittent low-frequency emission detected during initial scan (0.7 Hz, amplitude sub-threshold). Subsequent scans: null. Classified as instrument error.

Intake Recommendation: Object shows no active properties, no hazard indicators, no response to standard stimuli. Recommend classification as inert office equipment pending further review. Assigned to Sub-Level 3 storage, Shelf 14-C.

Notes: Object is warm to the touch. Maintenance has been advised. No explanation found.

Status: CLOSED — No Further Action Required.

Clive read it aloud.

His voice — that precise, noir cadence, every word chosen like a card drawn from a deck — did not waver. He read every line. The physical description. The resonance reading dismissed as instrument error. The recommendation. The classification. He read “inert office equipment” the way you’d read your own obituary written by someone who never met you.

When he finished, he was silent.

Not the retrieval-pause Pixel had learned to recognize. Not the processing-pause that preceded one of his observations. This was a different silence. Clive went absolutely still — no movement, no glow fluctuation, nothing. The Builder symbols on his chest dimmed. The blue-white light of his sphere head held steady at its lowest visible intensity, like a candle seen from very far away. His hands hung at his sides. His fedora cast its shadow.

The server room hummed around them. Mochi pulsed violet. Meatball, who had followed them in, pressed his body against Clive’s leg and stayed there.

Pixel counted the seconds because she didn’t know what else to do. She got to forty-seven before she couldn’t stand it anymore.

“Clive? Are you okay?”

The silence held for three more seconds. Then Clive’s head tilted — just slightly, the barest motion — and the glow brightened by one shade.

“They filed me under office supplies.” His voice was the same. Measured. Precise. “Accurate enough, at the time.”

The at the time carried weight. It sat in the air between them like something with mass — not an explanation, not a deflection, but a door left slightly open. An eight-hundred-year-old Builder Companion, designed to walk beside people through the vast and lonely spaces between frequencies, classified as inert. Shelved. Closed, no further action required.

Pixel wanted to say something. Something about how the DRM was wrong, how they didn’t understand what they were looking at, how Clive was more than a filing designation. But Clive adjusted his fedora — the gesture that meant moving on — and she understood that whatever he was feeling about Form 12-C, he was not ready to share it. Maybe not ever. Some things you carry, and carrying them is the whole story.

She put her hand on his shoulder. The alloy was warm.

“For what it’s worth,” she said, “your file was wrong.”

“Most files are.” A pause. The glow steadied. “That is why you became a journalist instead of staying a clerk.”

It was the closest thing to a compliment Clive had ever given her, and she chose not to ruin it by pointing that out.

The Access Logs

A1 had been quiet. When A1 was quiet, it meant something was being processed that required the full weight of a consciousness that had once navigated the spaces between frequencies. The espresso machine at the heart of the ship hummed at a lower register than usual — a deep, contemplative resonance that Meatball responded to by pressing harder against Clive’s leg.

“I have found something in the server’s access logs,” A1 said.

The display shifted. A table of entries — dates, timestamps, user IDs, files accessed. Thousands of lines spanning decades. But one pattern was unmistakable, because it repeated more than all the others combined.

KOWALSKI_B — Night Shift

Night after night after night. Twenty-three years of entries. Barry Kowalski, night security guard, Sub-Level 3, logging into a relay station data terminal he had no clearance to access, reading files he had no authorization to open. Frequency anomaly classifications. Builder structure reports. Signal pattern analyses. The deepest, most classified archives the DRM maintained — and Barry read them the way other people read the newspaper. Methodically. Completely. Every night.

“Twenty-three years,” Pixel said softly.

“He accessed every frequency classification file in the system,” A1 said. “Every anomaly report. Every Builder-adjacent signal analysis. The access pattern is not random. It is sequential. He read them in order, cross-referencing as he went. He was — ” A1 paused, and the word that followed carried a weight that made Pixel’s chest ache. ” — studying.”

Clive’s glow brightened. Not surprise — recognition. “Barry had a notebook,” he said. “Brown leather. He filled one every eighteen months. He never let me read them.”

“He was reading files about you,” Pixel said. “About things like you. Builder objects, frequency anomalies — he was trying to understand.”

“He understood,” Clive said. Simply. Certainly. With the bedrock conviction of someone who had spent twenty-three years sitting beside a man while he worked. “He understood before anyone.”

Pixel looked at the access logs scrolling down the screen. Twenty-three years of a night guard reading files by the light of a terminal he wasn’t supposed to use, sitting next to a small robot he’d given a fedora, filling brown leather notebooks with careful handwriting, asking questions no one in the DRM thought to ask because they’d already filed the answers under “closed — no further action required.”

She thought about her own four years at the DRM. Filing forms. Drinking A1’s coffee. Not asking questions. Barry had spent twenty-three years doing the opposite — asking every question — and the DRM had never noticed because he was night security and they never look at night security.

A1 produced a fresh espresso. The flavor was thick, almost syrupy — dark chocolate and something heavier underneath. The coffee A1 made when processing grief or loss. Pixel tasted it and set it down carefully, because some things A1 said through coffee were too honest to drink quickly.

Resolution

They worked in shifts. A1 archived everything — every file, every log, every scrap of data the relay station held. The servers gave up their contents in stuttering bursts as the power failed incrementally around them, lights dying corridor by corridor, the hum of machinery dropping in pitch as systems shut down one by one.

Pixel catalogued what she could. Frequency anomaly files that described structures she’d glimpsed through A1’s viewports — angular shapes, impossible architecture, things the DRM had detected and classified and filed away without ever going to look. Redacted reports on “Frequency Anomaly Classification Standards” that described phenomena the crew would later recognize — would later stand inside of — but which meant nothing to Pixel yet except the queasy certainty that the DRM had known about all of it. Everything. From the beginning.

Mochi had not stopped pulsing. The violet glow was steady now — not the blazing intensity of that first moment in the server room, but a persistent, deep resonance that Pixel could feel through the satchel like a second heartbeat. Whatever the servers held, whatever frequency data was stored in their dying circuits, Mochi was reading it. Not understanding it — Mochi did not understand. Mochi was a vessel carrying a memory she did not know she had, responding to resonance the way a compass responds to north. But the reading was strong. Stronger than Pixel had ever felt.

She made a note. Filed it the way she filed everything Mochi did: observed, unexplained, important.

Clive moved through the station alone for a while. Pixel let him. She watched him through the corridor windows — a small, stocky figure with a glowing head and a fedora, walking the institutional hallways with the deliberate pace of someone visiting a place that had tried to erase him. He stopped at Shelf 14-C in Sub-Level 3 storage. His shelf. The place where he’d sat for — she didn’t know how long. Decades? Centuries? However long it took for a night guard named Barry to start talking to him.

He stood there for four minutes. Pixel counted. Then he adjusted his fedora and walked back.

Meatball met him at the corridor junction and walked beside him, matching his pace exactly, the way dogs do when they know someone needs company but not conversation.

In the server room, as A1 pulled the last files from the failing systems, Pixel noticed something on the data stream she hadn’t seen before. Buried beneath the DRM’s operational data, beneath the classified files and access logs and intake forms — a carrier signal. Not data. Not voice. A tone. Low, steady, threading underneath every transmission the relay station had ever made like a foundation beneath a building. It was old. Older than the DRM. Older than the relay station. A frequency that predated the institution by centuries, embedded in the infrastructure like a fossil in stone.

She almost mentioned it. But when she looked at Clive, standing in the doorway with his glow at a steady medium blue, she saw that his head was tilted at an angle she didn’t recognize. Not curiosity. Not consideration. Something else. Something private.

He heard it. The tone. The carrier frequency that was older than the DRM, older than Barry, older than everything in this station except Clive himself.

He did not mention it.

The lights failed in sequence — operations room first, then the corridors, then the server room. A1 confirmed the data transfer was complete. Pixel zipped her jacket over Mochi — still warm, still pulsing that deep violet — and called Meatball. Clive walked beside her through the darkening corridors without needing the light, his body casting its own faint blue-white glow on the walls, Builder symbols tracing patterns that the DRM had once classified as “decorative etching, purpose unknown.”

They sealed the airlock behind them. The docking collar retracted. A1 eased the ship away from the relay station with the gentle precision of a consciousness that understood what it meant to leave something behind.

Through the viewport, the station tumbled — a dark rectangle against the static-laced void, its last running light still blinking red at three-second intervals. Pixel watched. The light blinked. Blinked. Blinked.

Then it didn’t.

The station went dark. The DRM’s last outpost, its last automated voice, its last signal — gone. Swallowed by the Space the way everything eventually is, the way the DRM itself had been swallowed, leaving behind only the data they’d pulled and the forms they’d filed and the twenty-three years of access logs that proved one night guard had been paying closer attention than anyone knew.

A1 produced espresso. The flavor had shifted — nutty now, warm, hazelnut and something grounding. The coffee A1 made when content with the presence of the crew. When being a ship felt like being a home.

Pixel wrapped her hands around the mug and breathed in the steam.

“A1? That personnel file. Kowalski, B. Can you cross-reference it with anything else in the archive?”

“I have already begun.” A pause. “Barry Kowalski’s access pattern suggests a systematic investigation spanning two decades. He was not reading casually. He was building a map.”

“A map of what?”

“I do not yet have enough data to answer that precisely. But the files he accessed most frequently concern frequency signatures that do not correspond to any catalogued location.” Another pause — longer, the kind that meant A1 was choosing its next words with unusual care. “He was looking for something that the DRM had detected but could not find.”

Pixel looked at Clive. Clive was sitting on the console, feet planted, glow steady, fedora tilted at its usual angle. He was looking at the viewport where the relay station had been. There was nothing there now. Just the Space — vast, static-laced, humming with frequencies the DRM had spent ninety years trying to file into submission.

“Clive?”

“Hm.”

“Did Barry ever tell you what he was looking for?”

A pause. The longest she’d heard from him. His glow dimmed, brightened, dimmed again — searching, reaching, finding or not finding.

“He said he was following a signal.” Clive’s voice was quiet. Even for Clive. “He said it was the most important thing he’d ever heard.” A beat. The fedora adjusted. “He said when he found where it led, he would explain everything.”

“And did he?”

“He left before he could. He followed the signal.” A pause. “I intend to find him eventually.”

Meatball sighed from his spot against the espresso machine’s warm brass housing — the deep, settling sigh of a dog who knows the day is done even if the questions aren’t. His paws twitched. Dreaming of frequencies that felt like warmth.

Pixel opened her broadcast console. The signal array hummed, ready, reaching out into the Space the way it always did — a voice into the void, hoping someone was listening.

“This is Pixel Paradox, broadcasting from the Ephergent. Third dispatch. We just left the last DRM relay station in existence. It’s dark now. Backup power finally gave out. But before it did, we pulled the archives.” She paused. Took a breath. “The DRM knew more than they told us. More than they told anyone. They had files on frequencies we haven’t visited, structures we haven’t found, signal patterns they detected and classified and buried in filing cabinets. Ninety years of data, and they sat on it.”

She looked at Clive. He was watching her. His glow was steady.

“They also had files on my crew. On things they couldn’t explain. They took the most extraordinary objects and people and phenomena they’d ever encountered, and they filed them. Gave them form numbers. Stamped them ‘closed, no further action required.’ And moved on.”

A beat. Mochi pulsed warm against her hip.

“The DRM couldn’t see what was right in front of them. I think that’s why they fell. Not because reality broke — because they’d been filing reality instead of understanding it. And when the forms stopped being enough to hold it, everything came apart.”

She leaned back. The mug of espresso was warm in her hands. Hazelnut. Home.

“We have the data. We’ll keep looking. If you’re out there — stay tuned.”

She cut the signal. The Space held them in its vast, humming silence. Somewhere in the data A1 was processing, Barry Kowalski’s twenty-three years of questions waited to be understood. Somewhere in the frequency Clive had heard and not mentioned, something older than institutions pulsed — patient, enduring, unclassified.

Clive sat on the console and said nothing. The fedora cast its shadow. His glow was blue-white and steady, the color of Builder signal energy, the color of something that had been filed under “inert” and was — quietly, deliberately, in its own time — proving the form wrong.

Character Focus

Pixel Paradox: The DRM was her world for four years, and finding its skeleton drifting through the Space forces her to confront what she was part of. She recognizes the filing system. She knows the form numbers. She used the same color-coded folders. The relay station is a mirror — showing her what happens when you file things instead of understanding them. Her broadcast this episode is sharper than the first two — less raw panic, more focused anger. She’s becoming a journalist. The Barry discovery opens a door she didn’t know existed: the DRM had people inside it who were doing exactly what she’s doing now, decades before she started. She’s not the first. That’s both comforting and terrifying.

Clive: Form 12-C is the episode’s emotional center. An eight-hundred-year-old Builder Companion, designed to guide people through the spaces between frequencies, reduced to a line item on an intake form. “Inert office equipment.” The gap between what Clive is and what the DRM saw him as is the gap between filing and understanding — the episode’s central theme. His silence after reading the form is the most important silence of the season so far. His “at the time” is a thread the audience will pull on for two more seasons. And the carrier tone — the frequency he hears and does not mention — is the first sign that Clive is hearing things the crew cannot. He is beginning to remember, even if he does not know it yet.

A1: Quiet this episode, which means A1 is processing something significant. The coffee tells the story — bright and curious at the start, cold when the Form 12-C appears, thick with grief over Barry’s access logs, warm and grounding by the end. A1 recognized the relay station’s data architecture as “familiar,” which is a word A1 does not use casually. The DRM built their systems on foundations they didn’t understand, using principles that originated somewhere A1 remembers but hasn’t named.

Mochi: The server room resonance is Mochi’s strongest reaction in the series so far. The violet glow, the heat, the way the servers themselves responded — this is data, not emotion. Mochi’s dormant crystal is reading frequency information stored in the DRM’s oldest archives, information that predates the DRM by centuries. The audience reads it as atmosphere. It is a Keeper recognizing the signal she was built to carry.

Meatball: The emotional weathervane, as always. His attentiveness in the operations room, his body pressed against Clive during the Form 12-C silence, his matched pace in the corridor afterward — Meatball reads the crew’s frequency, and this episode’s frequency is grief and recognition and the quiet weight of things that were hidden too long. His sigh at the end is the episode’s emotional punctuation: the day is done. The questions remain.
