Ruins, Ripples, and Really Nervous Dinosaurs

Pixel-Voice Synopsis

So you know that thing where you leave home in a hurry and then come back hoping it was all a bad dream? Yeah. We pointed the lifeboat back at Prime Material — home, our world, the normal one — and what we found was… not great. Not apocalyptic, exactly. More like that feeling when you come back from vacation and find out your apartment flooded, your landlord skipped town, and the laws of physics filed for early retirement.

The DRM headquarters — the boring government building where I spent four years filing forms about things I now realize were WAY more significant than anyone told me — was ruins. Just… rubble, sections of it phasing in and out of existence, surrounded by caution tape that itself was flickering. And already, already, opportunists had rolled in with folding tables selling “stability patches” and “frequency insurance” to panicked civilians. Because of course they had. The world starts breaking and someone’s got a side hustle within the hour.

But here’s the thing that broke my brain: the sub-basements. The DRM headquarters I knew was four floors of fluorescent lighting and bad coffee. Turns out, underneath that, there were SEVEN levels of hidden labs, classified archives, and technology that shouldn’t exist. Documents about “cross-frequency transit.” Research into “frequency manipulation.” Schematics for devices I filed paperwork about without ever knowing what they actually did. My employer — my boring, bureaucratic, form-in-triplicate employer — was hiding THE EXISTENCE OF OTHER FREQUENCIES. And I was cataloguing anomaly reports about them like they were parking violations.

Meanwhile, the city was falling apart. Buildings flickering between states — solid one moment, translucent the next, occasionally showing glimpses of impossible places bleeding through. Streets that didn’t obey normal geometry anymore. Traffic lights cycling through colors that don’t exist. Ordinary people panicking because their morning commute now includes a left turn through a hallway that shouldn’t be there. The financial markets were in freefall — not because of some exotic currency crisis, but because banks can’t function when their server rooms keep phasing out of existence. Infrastructure breaking. ATMs dispensing error codes. The stock exchange halted because the building itself couldn’t decide what floor it was on.

And that’s when it hit me: nobody is coming to fix this. The DRM is gone. The people who apparently knew about all of this — who were HIDING all of this — have left the building. And we’re on our own.

Narrative Structure

Opening

The lifeboat shudders through the phase-shift — a gut-twisting lurch that Pixel is learning to brace for, though Meatball never quite manages it and slides across the floor every time. The scratched porthole fills with light as Prime Material reasserts itself around them: the familiar skyline, glass office towers, the grey-blue haze of a city that was perfectly ordinary forty-eight hours ago.

But something’s wrong. The skyline stutters. Buildings flicker — their facades shimmering, going translucent for a heartbeat, revealing impossible geometries behind them before snapping back to normal. A high-rise downtown seems to exist in two places at once. The streets below are crowded but chaotic, traffic lights cycling through colors that shouldn’t exist, car GPS systems rerouting frantically as roads rearrange themselves in real time. People on sidewalks stopping, staring, filming with their phones, running.

A1’s holographic projection scans the scene from the porthole. “Significant frequency instability across the Prime Material substrate. The reality rupture appears to have destabilized the local frequency membrane. These ripples will—” He stops. Recalibrates. Something crosses his projection — surprise, maybe. Or recognition. “These are frequency-based ripples. Other frequencies are bleeding through the membrane. That shouldn’t — I shouldn’t know that term.” He pauses, processing something buried deep in his compressed memory. “The DRM headquarters is… I recommend preparing yourself, Pixel.”

They set the lifeboat down in a public park near the DRM district. The park fountain — usually a simple municipal water feature — is sputtering, its water occasionally freezing mid-arc or flowing upward, defying gravity in stuttering bursts.

Pixel steps out. Meatball follows, nose working overtime, tail low. Clive walks beside her, his small legs keeping pace, sphere head glowing a quiet blue-white. Occasionally he pauses, tilts his head at an angle Pixel has learned to read as consideration, and says nothing. She reads the silences. BAD. WORSE. MUCH WORSE. She doesn’t need him to spell it out with light patterns anymore.

In her jacket pocket, against her hip, Mochi is warm. Pixel notices. She always notices now, but she’s learned not to ask.

The DRM headquarters is a ruin.

Escalation

Not a clean ruin — not collapsed neatly like a demolished building. The DRM building is arguing with itself. Sections of wall phase in and out of visibility, showing rooms that shouldn’t exist stacked behind the ordinary offices Pixel remembers. Filing cabinets hang suspended in mid-air, their contents drifting into the breeze as loose forms — Form 19-Qs and Anomaly Reports and Frequency Variance Logs — scatter across the ruins like bureaucratic confetti.

A perimeter of DRM caution tape surrounds the site, but the tape itself is unstable, its warning text shifting: “CAUTION: STRUCTURAL DAMAGE” becomes “CAUTION: REALITY DISRUPTION” becomes “NOTICE: FREQUENCY BREACH — AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY” — warnings that the pre-rupture DRM would never have displayed publicly.

But it’s what’s below the building that stops Pixel cold. The collapse has torn open the sub-basements — seven levels of hidden infrastructure that the ordinary DRM headquarters was sitting on top of like a boring hat on an impossible head. Labs full of technology Pixel has never seen. Monitoring equipment that was clearly tracking something far beyond building code violations. Classified archives with documents stamped “FREQUENCY SENSITIVE — DIRECTOR LEVEL ONLY.” Schematics for devices that manipulate the fabric of reality itself. Maps of places labeled “Nocturne Aeturnus,” “Cogsworth Cogitarium,” “The Verdant Deep” — other frequencies, other worlds, catalogued and monitored and hidden behind four floors of fluorescent lighting and mandatory fun committees.

Pixel stands in the ruins of a lab she never knew existed, holding a clipboard she recognizes — her own handwriting, her own anomaly reports — filed into a system that was feeding data to this. She filed Form 19-Q about “minor frequency variance in Sector 7” and it went HERE. To a secret lab studying cross-frequency physics. Her hands shake.

Former DRM employees wander the perimeter, dazed. Some are in uniform. Some are in civilian clothes, having been off-shift when the rupture hit. Most of them look as blindsided as Pixel — fellow paper-pushers who had no idea what was underneath them. But a few — a handful — have a different expression. Not shock. Guilt. Pixel files that observation away.

On a personnel board near the ruined HR office, among the lost ID badges and abandoned access cards, Pixel glimpses a name she doesn’t yet recognize: B. KOWALSKI — Night Security, Sub-Level 3, 23 Years Service. Someone else was here before her. Someone else worked nights in a place they didn’t understand. She files it. Noted. Not relevant. Not yet.

But nature abhors a vacuum, and so does commerce.

The reality entrepreneurs have already arrived. Folding tables and popup tents line the streets around the DRM ruins, staffed by fast-talking vendors selling “Frequency Stabilization Patches” — adhesive strips that allegedly smooth out local reality ripples. “Guaranteed for twenty-four hours!” shouts a vendor with a megaphone and a smile that’s slightly too wide. “Stick it on your wall, your desk, your forehead — keeps reality where it belongs!” The patches glow faintly, and Pixel’s analytical instincts tell her they’re doing something — but it’s the equivalent of putting a band-aid on a broken bone.

Others are selling “Ripple Insurance,” “Reality Continuity Plans,” and — Pixel’s personal favorite — “Commemorative DRM Collapse Souvenir Snow Globes” that contain actual fragments of unstable reality. Shake it and you see a tiny DRM headquarters collapse in miniature. Tasteful.

The real crisis is everywhere. The financial district is in meltdown — not some exotic currency crash, but the fundamental breakdown of infrastructure. Bank servers keep phasing out of existence. ATMs display error messages in languages that don’t exist. The stock exchange building itself can’t maintain consistent geometry, its floors shuffling like a deck of cards, and trading has been halted indefinitely. On the steps of the Exchange, a suited executive screams into a phone that keeps turning transparent in his hand. Behind him, the digital ticker — when it’s visible — shows nothing but red.

And in the gaps where reality is thinnest, things are bleeding through. Shapes that don’t belong — impossible creatures glimpsed for a moment where the frequency membrane has worn thin. A shadow with too many legs crosses a wall and vanishes. Something that might be a bird but has geometry instead of feathers circles a church steeple before dissolving. People are filming everything on phones that intermittently refuse to function. Social media, when it works, is a firehose of panic.

Deep Basement — Clive’s Doorway

Pixel descends.

The sub-basements go down farther than she expected — past Sub-Level 1, past Sub-Level 2, into levels that don’t have names on the emergency signage because nobody was supposed to know they existed. Sub-Level 3. Sub-Level 4. Sub-Level 5. The air changes with each level — warmer, older, humming with equipment that Pixel doesn’t recognize.

Clive stops at a doorway.

It’s nothing special — a standard DRM door frame, institutional grey, a sign that reads “SUB-LEVEL 7 — AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY” in letters that flicker with the power fluctuations. But Clive stops. His sphere head tilts up toward the frame. His glow brightens — just for a moment, a blue-white pulse that Pixel might have missed if she wasn’t watching.

“Efficient architecture,” Clive says. His voice is quiet. Almost to himself. “Someone designed this to last.”

Pixel looks at the door. Looks at Clive. “You know this place?”

A pause. The longest pause she’s heard from him since the building collapse. His glow shifts — dim, bright, dim again — like something is being retrieved from a deep archive.

“In a manner of speaking.” He doesn’t elaborate. He never elaborates. He adjusts his fedora and walks through the doorway like he’s done it a thousand times before.

Pixel follows. She doesn’t ask again.

In the deepest sub-basement, the oldest equipment hums. Monitors that predate digital displays — CRT screens in racks, oscilloscopes with analog dials, frequency monitors with readouts in units Pixel doesn’t recognize. Builder-derived, A1 would say if he were here. Builder-adjacent. Old. Ancient, maybe, though Pixel doesn’t have a reference for what ancient technology looks like in a government building.

And Mochi — in Pixel’s jacket pocket, suddenly hot. Not painful. Not alarming. Just present — a pulse of warmth that radiates through the fabric and into her hip, steady and insistent, like a second heartbeat keeping time with something in the walls.

Pixel doesn’t say anything. She unzips her jacket slightly, looks at Mochi’s dome visible in the pocket’s shadow, and feels the warmth against her fingers.

“Yeah,” she says quietly. “I don’t know either.”

The hum from the deepest corridor is too low for human hearing. But Meatball’s ears swivel forward. He sits. He listens. His tail stills.

A1, speaking through the lifeboat comm from above: “Pixel. The equipment in Sub-Level 7 is transmitting. Low-frequency carrier signal. I am logging it.”

Pixel looks at Clive. Clive’s sphere is steady. He’s not looking at the equipment. He’s looking at a point on the far wall — a maintenance hatch, sealed, with a small handwritten label in handwriting that looks like it predates the building.

She’ll ask about that later too.

Climax

Pixel finds a relatively stable spot on the steps of the ruined DRM headquarters — poetic, she thinks — and sets up the lifeboat’s comm array for a proper broadcast. Not the desperate, static-laced first transmission from the Space. This time, she’s doing it right. This is journalism. This is the first dispatch from the new reality.

A1 extends his holographic range (it’s grown again, Pixel notices — he can project nearly a meter from the espresso machine now, and he doesn’t seem to realize it) and helps her calibrate the signal. Clive positions himself on the broadcast console, ready to provide real-time editorial commentary. His sphere glows soft blue-white, patient, watchful.

Meatball sits beside her, facing the crowd, and does something unexpected: he howls. Not a scared howl, not a warning. A long, low, mournful sound that cuts through the noise of the plaza. People stop and turn. It’s the kind of sound that says listen.

Pixel takes the cue.

“This is Pixel Paradox, broadcasting from the ruins of the Department of Reality Maintenance, Prime Material. Second dispatch. If you heard the first one — thank you for listening. If this is new to you — welcome to the worst day in history, and I’m sorry you had to find out this way.”

She describes what she sees: the ruins, the ripples, the entrepreneurs, the panicking city. Then she takes a breath and says the part that matters.

“I worked at the DRM for four years. I filed paperwork. I catalogued anomaly reports. I thought I worked for a boring government agency that handled building inspections and zoning compliance. I was wrong.” Her voice steadies. “Underneath the DRM headquarters — underneath the break room where I ate lunch every day — there are seven floors of hidden laboratories. Classified archives. Technology that shouldn’t exist. Documents describing other frequencies. Other worlds, with names and coordinates, monitored in secret by the organization that told me I was filing noise complaints.”

She holds up a document — one of dozens she pulled from the ruins. “This is Form 19-Q. MY Form 19-Q. My handwriting. I filed this report about a ‘minor frequency variance’ in Sector 7 three months ago. I thought it was routine. It was being fed into a system tracking frequency stability across multiple realities. I didn’t know. Most of us didn’t know.”

“The DRM is gone. I want to be clear about that. Not coming back, not regrouping. And what they were hiding — these frequencies, these other worlds — they’re REAL. The rupture didn’t just destroy a building. It tore the walls between worlds. And now everything on the other side is bleeding through.”

She pauses. Clive’s sphere pulses softly: HOPE. OFFER HOPE.

“But here’s the thing. We’re still here. Whatever is happening — whatever these frequencies are, whatever the DRM was keeping from us — reality is messy and glitchy and the markets are having a really bad day, but we’re still standing. And as long as I’ve got this comm array and this lifeboat and the world’s best espresso machine, I’m going to keep telling you what’s happening. What I’m finding. What the DRM knew and didn’t tell us.”

A1’s projection shimmers beside her. He says nothing, but his holographic form is clearer than it’s ever been. Sharper. More present.

“This is The Ephergent. Dispatch two. Prime Material is hurting, but it’s standing. We’re heading out to see what else is out there — these other frequencies, these other worlds. If they’re real, someone needs to go look. If you’re out there — stay tuned.”

Resolution

After the broadcast, Pixel and the crew spend the rest of the cycle doing what they can. They barter for supplies — power cells for the lifeboat, nutrient packs, a replacement air filter that doesn’t smell like a copier’s funeral. The economy is chaos, but Pixel’s broadcast has earned them something more valuable: a few people recognize her voice from the first transmission and press supplies into her hands. “Heard you from the static,” a dockworker says, a burly guy with calloused hands and a thousand-yard stare. “Thought I was losing my mind. Glad someone’s actually saying what’s happening.”

A1 quietly catalogs everything he can about the rupture’s effects on Prime Material. He’s been running calculations that he hasn’t shared with Pixel — calculations about the rate of frequency drift, the propagation of instability, the growing silence from places he shouldn’t know exist. The math isn’t good. Something he’s calling “the Drift” — the word surfaces from somewhere deep in his compressed memory, surprising even him — is accelerating. The barriers between frequencies are thinning. And he can feel it, which frightens him more than the math.

Clive spends an hour at the ruins of the DRM records office, tracing light-patterns across the debris in signals too complex for Pixel to read from a distance. But his posture is different from the other mourning employees. He’s not confused. He’s not shocked. When she asks later, he simply pulses: PAYING RESPECTS. But Pixel notices he doesn’t pulse “I didn’t know.” He doesn’t pulse anything about the hidden labs being a surprise. She files that observation next to the guilty faces she saw earlier.

Before they leave, Pixel walks through the financial district one more time. The suited executive is still on the steps, hoarse now, still insisting the fundamentals are sound to anyone who’ll listen. A group of reality entrepreneurs have set up a more permanent-looking booth, selling “Premium Stability Subscriptions.” The corner diner — the one that somehow stayed open through every crisis the city ever had — is still open, its neon sign flickering but defiant. A hand-scrawled sign in the window reads: “REALITY MAY BE BROKEN BUT THE COFFEE IS HOT.”

Pixel smiles. Files that image in her mind for later.

They depart Prime Material as the light fades, the lifeboat groaning with the strain of another phase-shift. Through the porthole, Pixel watches the city skyline recede — glitchy, wounded, but still blazing. A normal city that will never be normal again.

A1 speaks quietly. “The other frequencies — the other worlds — will be experiencing similar disruptions. Possibly worse, depending on their proximity to the rupture’s epicenter.” He pauses. “I don’t know how I know that.”

Pixel nods. “Then that’s where we go. Every frequency, A1. If they’re real, we go look at every single one.”

Clive pulses softly: GOOD KID.

Meatball sighs and lays his head on his paws, already dreaming of places that feel like warmth.

In her pocket, Mochi is warm. Pixel rests her hand over the pocket. Doesn’t say anything. Just rests it there.

The lifeboat enters the Space between. The dial turns. The next frequency awaits.

Character Focus

Pixel Paradox: This episode cements her transition from analyst to correspondent — and from unwitting cog to furious truth-seeker. The first broadcast from the Space was desperate — this one is deliberate. She’s choosing this role, not falling into it. Her analytical skills are sharpening under pressure: she can see the stability patches are useless, can read the depth of the financial instability, can sense which buildings are close to phase-collapsing. But the real transformation is emotional — the betrayal of discovering her employer was hiding the existence of other frequencies while she filed paperwork about them. That anger fuels her journalism. She’s also learning the weight of being the only voice — when you’re the one broadcasting, people start looking at you for answers you don’t have.

A1: Growing. Literally. His holographic range keeps expanding, and he’s running calculations he’s not sharing. The rupture cracked open something in his compressed form, and knowledge is leaking out — terms he shouldn’t know, understanding of frequency physics that surprises him as much as anyone. He’s beginning to sense the scope of what he once was — vast, ancient, something that navigated the spaces between worlds. But this awareness is new and unsettling, not the comfortable expansion of known capabilities. His protectiveness toward Pixel is deepening; he doesn’t just want to keep her alive, he wants to keep her signal alive.

Clive: Paying respects at the DRM ruins is Clive’s emotional core this episode — but it reads differently under scrutiny. He spent years in that building. His grief manifests as precise, measured silences that Pixel has learned to read. He isn’t surprised by the hidden labs. He’s mourning the exposure of secrets he was sworn to keep, not the discovery of them. His editorial instinct — pulsing HOPE. OFFER HOPE. during Pixel’s broadcast — shows he understands the power of narrative even through his laconic exterior. The doorway pause in Sub-Level 7 — his glow brightening at a door he shouldn’t recognize — is the seed. What did Clive know, and when?

Mochi: In Pixel’s jacket pocket, warm and present, reacting to Builder-derived equipment in the deepest sub-basement. Mochi doesn’t speak, doesn’t explain, doesn’t react beyond warmth. But the warmth is specific — stronger near equipment that hums with frequencies older than the DRM. Pixel notices. She files it. Observed. Unexplained. Important.

Meatball: The emotional weathervane. His howl in the plaza — cutting through chaos, making people stop and listen — establishes him as more than comic relief. He perceives frequencies as emotion, and Prime Material right now feels like grief and stubborn hope tangled together. In the deepest sub-basement, his ears swivel toward a hum too low for human hearing — the Ephergent Frequency, buried in the equipment’s carrier signal, audible only to those tuned to receive it. His simple presence grounds the crew. When Meatball curls up and sleeps, it means: we’re okay for now.
